Context

March 2024 — public S3 exposure

~18,400 customer configuration records exposed (no biometric templates). Public coverage was severe because our product is trust itself.

August 2024 — stolen developer key

A shared SSH key was used to reach a staging node. Detection took 81 hours — that number is now the baseline every quarter is measured against.

The aftermath

Five senior security staff departed. Public trust collapsed. The Series B closed with governance conditions: independent directors, an Audit Committee, Internal Audit.

Eighteen months into the rebuild, the honest question is not "are we fixed?" — it is "do we know, in dollars, where we are still exposed, and is that exposure falling every quarter?"

The plan

We will run risk as a daily operation — a Risk Operations Center, not a bigger alarm room.

What a SOC answers

"Is someone attacking us right now?" Reactive, alert-driven, measured in response minutes. We keep this capability — Nadia Khan's team — and we do not build a second one.

What the ROC answers

"Where are we most exposed, and how do we systematically reduce that exposure — in dollars?" Proactive, risk-driven, measured in exposure reduced per dollar spent. This is the operating system for everything on slide 5.

  • One queue: every risk signal — vulnerabilities, misconfigurations, threat intelligence, supplier findings — lands in one prioritized register, weighted by business impact, not raw severity. If everything is critical, nothing is.
  • One language: exposure expressed as probable loss in dollars, calibrated against the risk appetite already written into our insurance limits.
  • One cadence: weekly prioritize-validate-remediate cycles replace the annual assessment ritual; the quarterly CSF score becomes a read-out, not an event.

Full process: "Establishing ROC Principles at Alma Security" (companion document)

Roadmap

Four steps over 15 months — each with an exit test you can hold us to.

Step 1 · Months 1–3 — See everything once

Unified asset inventory with owners and criticality (retires R4, funded by the ASM project). Exit: ≥70% coverage, orphan assets <10%, first risk baseline published.

Step 2 · Months 3–8 — Operate weekly

Standing prioritize-validate-remediate cycle; engineering squads own their queues; first dollar estimates on top scenarios. Exit: fixes outpace new findings two months running.

Step 3 · Months 9–14 — Price it

Loss-versus-appetite curves for the top five scenarios; every investment proposed with projected risk reduction; automation drafts, humans approve. Exit: FY2027 budget argued in dollars-at-risk.

Step 4 · Month 15+ — Mature

Quarterly board pack fully in dollars; evaluate managed 24/7 augmentation instead of overnight hiring. Exit: reporting you can read without a glossary, every quarter.

People: no new department. The vulnerability-management and GRC functions become the ROC seed team; one planned backfill is redirected to a data-pipeline engineer.

Exit criteria tracked in the risk operations review · maturity target: "Defined" by end-2027

Governance

Your quarterly view becomes three questions with numeric answers.

Question 1

How much risk do we have?

Value at risk, in dollars, against the appetite line derived from our insurance limits — per business area.

Question 2

What is the plan?

How much we mitigate, accept, and transfer — and what each treatment costs.

Question 3

Is it working?

Exposure trend, fix-rate versus new-finding rate, and return per dollar of remediation.

The standard we are adopting: every page of the quarterly pack readable by a director with no cybersecurity background. If a page needs a glossary, the page has failed — not the director.

Six-section quarterly format · monthly detail flows to the Audit Committee, not the full board
